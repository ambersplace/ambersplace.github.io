Sky Edwards Photography@SkyEdwardsPhotography8/10/2024, 1:03 PM

One way to think of this is that a longer lens is zoomed in on the blur.

If we imagine a 50mm and 400mm taking a photo at say, f/5.6. The background will be blurred the same amount because it's the same aperture. But they will look completely different because the 400mm has taken a tiny bit of that blurry background and stretched it across the whole frame. The boke is in a sense now bigger, zoomed in on. So you get a far greater apparent background blur and far smaller effective depth of field by increasing the focal length.

Sky Edwards Photography@SkyEdwardsPhotography8/10/2024, 12:51 PM

I believe the central point is about avoiding perspective distortion: The extreme of that is fisheye photography, which will give your subject a giant nose and weird proportions.

Less extreme than that: 50mm (full frame) or 35mm (crop) is usually ok if you aren't too close, great for full body portraits. But if you do a head shot with this, you will often give your subject a subtly larger nose or chin or whatever body part is closest to the lens. This is sometimes fine, potentially useful if your subject is self conscious of a small nose or big ears. But instead, a longer focal length avoids the problem entirely.

So 85mm (ff) or 50mm(crop) is a popular length if you are doing head or head and torso shots.

135 seems a bit too long for convenience, but gives basically certainty that you won't distort your subject.

Also unrelated to that: at 135mm you are less likely to have trouble with your shadow being in the frame.

Other important factors others have mentioned:
-Longer focal length lets you choose a nice small area of background, if you're in an area with nice bits and ugly bits.
-Depth of field is controlled by aperture, format/sensor size, and focal length. If you can't change to a larger film format, the best ways to make a smaller depth of field are a longer focal length or a larger aperture. I feel like my 400 f/5.6 has vaguely similar boke to a 50 f/1.8.

I guess that's why 135 f/2.8 is so popular: not too big or expensive while still achieving all of that.
